Watch a twelve-year-old navigate across a city.

They open Maps, type the destination, follow the blue arrow. They have never read a street sign for navigation. They don't build a mental map; they outsource the map-building to a system that does it more accurately and efficiently than they could. They arrive at their destination.

Has something been lost? Maybe. Has something been gained? Also maybe. The twelve-year-old can navigate successfully in any city on earth with no local knowledge — a capability their parents didn't have at their age. But they cannot navigate in a city without signal. And they haven't built the spatial reasoning that comes from having to navigate without a crutch.

I am not here to tell you this is bad. I'm here to tell you the debate we're currently having about it is the wrong one.

## The wrong debate

The public conversation about children and technology is a debate about screen time. How many hours per day. Which apps are acceptable. Whether devices should be allowed in classrooms. The assumption is that "the screen" is the problem — that exposure to screens is intrinsically harmful and limiting exposure is intrinsically protective.

This is a debate about the container, not the contents.

What's actually happening is a shift in which cognitive functions we offload to external systems, and how deeply those systems are embedded in daily life. The screen is just the interface. The underlying question — the one we're not having — is: what happens to human cognitive development when the things we used to develop through practice are now handled by systems that do them better?

Navigation used to require spatial reasoning. We developed spatial reasoning partly because navigation demanded it. When navigation is handled by GPS, the developmental demand changes. This isn't obviously good or bad — it depends on how much spatial reasoning you need for other things, and what else fills the cognitive space that navigation used to occupy.

But we're not asking that question. We're arguing about screen time.

## A longer history of offloading

Cognitive offload is not a technology-era invention. It's a continuous feature of human civilization.

Writing was a cognitive offload. Instead of maintaining oral traditions in living memory, communities could externalize knowledge to marks on clay tablets. Critics at the time — Socrates is the famous example — worried that writing would weaken memory and produce people who knew how to look things up but couldn't actually think. He was partially right and mostly wrong: writing changed what memory was demanded, didn't eliminate the need for it, and enabled knowledge to accumulate across generations in ways oral culture couldn't.

Arithmetic is a cognitive offload. The calculator didn't create a generation of people who can't think mathematically; it shifted the demand toward mathematical reasoning and away from mechanical computation. We still teach arithmetic, but the reason has quietly changed: not because you'll do the computation by hand in adult life, but because understanding what computation means requires having done it.

Maps were a cognitive offload long before GPS. The question is never whether to offload — it's which cognitive functions are worth preserving as internal capacities, and which can be safely externalized without cost to the capacities that matter.

> The screen time debate avoids this question because answering it requires deciding what cognitive capacities are constitutive of a good life — and that is a philosophical question that our policy apparatus doesn't know how to handle.

## The difference now

There are two things that make the current wave of cognitive offloading different from writing or the calculator.

The first is the depth of the functions being offloaded. We're not just outsourcing arithmetic anymore. We're outsourcing pattern recognition, drafting, decision support, social interaction, emotional processing. These are not peripheral cognitive functions. They're close to the center of what we mean by human intelligence.

The second is the adaptivity of the systems. A map doesn't change based on how you use it. A GPS that learns your driving patterns and adjusts its suggestions is different. A language model that responds to your question, anticipates your needs, and provides increasingly tailored output is doing something that no previous tool has done: it's adapting to you in real time, which means your interaction with it shapes the output, which means the offload is more thorough and more invisible than previous offloads.

When navigation is adaptive — when the system learns where you go and routes you before you ask — the demand for human spatial reasoning drops further than it did with a static map. When writing assistance is adaptive — when the system suggests the next sentence based on your style — the demand for generating your own prose structure drops further than it did with a dictionary.

The question is not whether this is happening. It is. The question is whether we are making any choices about it.

## What I actually want to know

I have kids. I watch them interact with these systems. I see the capacities being developed and the ones being skipped.

I don't know which cognitive functions are constitutive of a flourishing life and which are just habits of a particular historical moment. I genuinely don't know whether spatial navigation is important to develop, or whether it's like cursive handwriting — a skill that mattered in a specific context and doesn't anymore.

What I do know is that we are not asking that question. The conversation about screen time is a proxy for anxiety about change, not a serious attempt to identify which cognitive capacities matter and why.

The serious question is: which capacities are so central to what it means to think, to create, to relate, to choose — that offloading them to a system would represent a meaningful loss of human experience? And for each of those capacities, are we building the developmental conditions that cultivate them?

That question doesn't get answered by setting a two-hour daily screen limit. It gets answered by actually deciding what education is for — which is a conversation we have been avoiding for as long as I've been paying attention.
