Ask a principal what school is for, and she'll give you something like this: to develop critical thinkers, to prepare engaged citizens, to foster a love of learning, to support students in becoming their best selves.

Ask the school's systems — its grading rubrics, its attendance policies, its course requirements, its counseling resources — and they'll give you a different answer: to rank students by cognitive aptitude and prepare the successful ones for college and the labor market.

These are not the same answer. And the distance between them is, I think, the central evasion in American education policy.

## Two answers running simultaneously

Every serious study of how schools actually function finds the same gap. The stated mission is about developing full human beings. The operational mission — the one reflected in what gets measured, what gets resourced, and what gets reported — is about producing and sorting workers.

This isn't an accident or a conspiracy. It is what happens when an institution built for one purpose is asked to describe itself using the vocabulary of a different purpose.

The modern public school system was deliberately designed for an industrial economy. Not as a side effect — as a core design goal. The Prussian education model that shaped American public schooling in the 19th century was explicit: create workers who could follow structured instruction, perform consistently under standardized conditions, and be sorted by aptitude so the most capable could be directed toward the most demanding roles.

The system worked. It is, in fact, one of the most successful institutional designs in human history at achieving its actual purpose. American schools sorted an industrial workforce for over a century. They produced the workers the economy needed, at the scale the economy needed, with a level of standardization that allowed employers to treat credentials as reliable signals of capability.

The problem isn't that schools failed. The problem is that what they were built to succeed at is changing.

## What schools are measuring

The core measurement apparatus of American education — standardized tests, GPA, course rigor, college acceptance rates — is measuring something specific: the ability to perform on demand, under time pressure, on tasks that have been standardized in advance.

This is a remarkably good measure of the cognitive capabilities that industrial and knowledge-economy jobs required: being able to learn procedures, apply them accurately, and demonstrate mastery in a setting where the parameters are defined by someone else.

> It is a remarkably poor measure of the cognitive capabilities that are becoming the scarce resource in a world where the standardizable tasks are increasingly performed by technological intelligence.

What's scarce now is not the ability to follow a structured procedure accurately. It's the ability to frame a problem that hasn't been framed before. To recognize that the AI's answer, though confidently stated, is missing something. To ask the next question — the one that doesn't appear in the training data. To make a judgment about what matters when the stakes are real and the answer isn't in the rubric.

Our measurement apparatus rewards students who master what has already been standardized. The economy is increasingly rewarding people who do things that haven't been standardized yet.

## The honest problem

Here is what makes this hard to fix: the measurement apparatus is not just measuring students. It is providing the signal that colleges use to make admissions decisions, that employers use to screen candidates, that families use to evaluate their children's trajectories, and that communities use to evaluate their schools.

You cannot simply change what schools measure without changing all of those downstream uses of the measurement. The GPA is load-bearing. It holds up a very large structure.

So when I say "school is measuring the wrong things," I am not saying schools could just switch to measuring different things tomorrow. I am saying that the measurement apparatus was built for a world that is changing, and the costs of rebuilding it are real and will be contested.

What I am saying is that we need to have the harder conversation — the one about what schools are actually supposed to produce, not the one about which version of the existing measurement system is slightly better.

## The question we've never answered

If schools are no longer primarily for producing workers — because the TI-mental inflection point means the economy increasingly doesn't need most people's labor to perform rote cognitive tasks — what are they for?

This question is not rhetorical. I don't have a fully formed answer. But I have a strong suspicion that the honest answer has to start somewhere like this: schools should produce people who know how to live — who have a sense of what they value, the capacity to pursue it, the judgment to navigate difficulty, and the resilience to adapt when circumstances change.

That's a very different design brief than "produce and sort workers." It requires different metrics, different structures, different definitions of success. It probably requires smaller schools, more relational teaching, more time spent on genuine inquiry and less on standardized preparation. It almost certainly produces students who are harder to rank against each other on a single dimension.

It is also, I think, more honest about what human flourishing actually requires than anything the current system was designed to deliver.

Every major education reform fight for the last fifty years has been a fight about which version of the existing system to prefer: more testing or less, more school choice or less, more vocational training or more college prep. These are arguments about how to optimize the industrial design.

The argument we haven't had — the one that the TI-mental inflection point is forcing us toward — is about whether the industrial design is still the right design for the world we're actually entering.

The window for that argument is open. The question is whether we use it.
