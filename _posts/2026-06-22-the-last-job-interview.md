In 2018, a major law firm hired a team of junior associates to review 1.5 million documents in a pharmaceutical patent litigation. The work was exactly what law school trains you for: reading carefully, flagging relevance, categorizing evidence, building toward an argument. They charged by the hour. It took months.

That same case could be run today with an e-discovery AI system in roughly 72 hours, at a fraction of the cost, with accuracy that meets or exceeds human review on most categories of document.

The junior associates aren't unemployed. But the work that justified their existence at that firm — the work they interviewed for, the work they were trained to do — is no longer the bottleneck. Something faster and cheaper does it now.

This is not a story about artificial intelligence being impressive. It is a story about what a job interview is actually measuring.

## What the interview was for

A job interview is a prediction mechanism. The interviewer is trying to predict who will be valuable to the organization. The interview tests the capacities that have historically made someone valuable: analytical skill, communication, judgment under pressure, domain knowledge.

The problem is that what made someone valuable in 2015 is not identical to what will make them valuable in 2030. And the gap is widening faster than our hiring practices are updating.

Legal document review was never glamorous work. Associates did it because it was the path to more interesting work, and because someone had to do it. Now that "someone" is a language model trained on millions of legal documents. The interview question "can you handle high-volume document review?" is measuring a skill that has already been substantially automated. The interview doesn't know that yet.

This is the pattern. Radiology fellowship programs still train physicians to read chest X-rays manually — the foundational skill of the field. The AI systems that now match or outperform radiologists on specific diagnostic tasks were deployed while the training programs were still running. The interview asks for manual reading fluency. The job increasingly requires knowing how to supervise, validate, and interrogate the AI's output.

The interview and the job have quietly diverged.

## The AlphaFold template

In 2020, DeepMind released a system that predicted the structures of proteins from their amino acid sequences with accuracy that matched expensive experimental methods. By 2022, it had predicted the structures of more than 200 million proteins — essentially every protein known to science. The work that had occupied structural biologists for decades collapsed from months-per-structure to seconds.

The structural biologists did not disappear. They pivoted. They asked bigger questions, designed more ambitious experiments, pursued the things that AlphaFold couldn't do — the things that required judgment, creativity, and scientific intuition about what mattered.

But not everyone made the pivot. The researchers who had spent years mastering crystallography techniques faced a version of the same problem as the e-discovery associates: their core technical skill had been absorbed by the system. They could adapt to a new role or find the field had moved past them.

Here is what I want you to notice: the pivot wasn't optional, and it wasn't announced. There was no transition program. The researchers who thrived were the ones who recognized what had changed and moved toward the new bottleneck — not the ones who doubled down on the old expertise.

## What the new interview should be

If the old interview tested your ability to do the task, the new interview should test your ability to work alongside the system that does the task.

This is harder to interview for. It requires knowing what the system gets wrong, how to catch its errors, how to ask it better questions, how to recognize when the task is outside its competence. It requires judgment about when to trust the output and when to verify it. It requires the kind of metacognitive awareness that takes years to develop and is very difficult to fake in forty-five minutes.

Most hiring processes aren't testing for this yet. The questions are still structured around the old version of the role. The credentials being screened still represent mastery of skills that are, in many fields, no longer the limiting factor.

## The harder question

> When we train a generation for jobs that won't exist, and then tell them they failed to compete, who is actually failing?

The e-discovery associates didn't do anything wrong. They went to good schools, took the right courses, built the right skills, passed the bar. They were prepared for the job as it existed when they started preparing.

The job changed faster than the preparation system could update.

This is the central tension I keep returning to. Individuals are being evaluated by institutions that haven't updated their assumptions. Students are trained for roles that are already shifting. The gap between what the system teaches and what the economy actually rewards is growing.

The associates will adapt, most of them. They'll learn the new tools, reposition toward the work that requires human judgment, survive. But they'll do it despite their training, not because of it. And the next cohort coming through the same programs is getting the same preparation they got — optimized for a version of the job that existed five years ago.

The last job interview isn't the one where a human is replaced by a machine. It's the one where we realize the interview was already testing for the wrong things, and we haven't changed it.
