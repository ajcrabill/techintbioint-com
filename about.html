About

Who writes this and why.

I'm AJ Crabill. I write TI×BI because I think we are approaching two inflection points that will redefine what it means to be human in an economy — and we are not remotely prepared for either one.

The first is when technological intelligence can outperform biological intelligence in more than 80% of the mental labor the economy relied on five years earlier. The second is when embodied technological intelligence — robots, androids, whatever form factor wins — can do the same for physical labor. I think both will arrive by 2050. Every child in school today will face them.

That sounds alarming. It isn't meant to. I'm not predicting catastrophe. I'm predicting displacement — and displacement is only catastrophic if we keep organizing society around assumptions that are about to stop being true.

The goal of this site is to think clearly about what's actually happening, without either the techno-utopian promise that AI solves everything or the doom-loop fear that we're all obsolete. Both are lazy. The situation calls for something harder: honest analysis and then choosing better.

Background

I grew up in foster care, attended eleven different schools before graduating high school, and came out of that experience with a particular way of seeing institutions — from the outside, then the inside, then the outside again. I went on to build and run a tech startup, which taught me what it looks like when a new tool changes the economics of an entire sector in a short window of time.

Outside of writing, I spend time coding, cooking vegan food, riding an electric unicycle, and mentoring young people who remind me why this work matters. I've helped raise five young people and mentored dozens more. The question of what kind of world they'll inherit is not abstract to me.

Why "TI×BI"?

Most conversations about artificial intelligence use terms that sneak in assumptions. "Artificial" implies fake. "AGI" implies a specific (contested) theory about consciousness. I prefer "technological intelligence" because it names what the thing actually is: intelligence implemented through technology, neither fake nor necessarily conscious, but capable — and increasingly, more capable than us at a widening set of tasks.

"Biological intelligence" is what we have: wetware, evolved over millions of years, adapted for a world that no longer exists in quite the same form. The × between them is not a threat or a merger. It's a collision that's already happening, and the essays here are my attempt to see it clearly.

Format

One essay per week, published every Monday. I write about what I'm thinking through, which means the essays follow a line of inquiry rather than a content calendar. Some will be wrong. I'll update my thinking when they are.
